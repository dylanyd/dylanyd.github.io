Autonomous driving specialization: Introduction to self-driving

Terms and Definitions

Driving task

  1. Perceiving the environment
  2. Planning how to reach from A to B
  3. Controlling the vehicle

Operational Design Domain (ODD)

ODD: description of the specific operating domain(s) in which an automated function or system is designed to properly operate, including but not limited to roadway types, speed range, environmental conditions (weather, daytime/nighttime, etc.), and other domain constraints

Components of the driving task

  1. Object and Event Detection and Response (OEDR)

  2. Planning

    1. Long term
    2. Short term

  3. Control

    1. Lateral control: the task of steering and navigating laterally on the road.
    2. Longitudinal control: control the position or velocity of the car (braking and acceleration) along the roadway.

Levels of autonomous driving

  1. L1: Driving Assistance
    • Adaptive Cruise Control to control speed
    • Lane keeping assistance to help stay in lane
  2. L2: Partial Driving Automation
  3. L3: Conditional Driving Automation
  4. L4: High Driving Automation
  5. L5: Full Driving Automation

Perception

Perception: Make sense of the environment and ourselves by identification and understanding motion of objects in the driving environment.

Goals of perception

  1. Static objects

    • Road and lane markings
    • Curbs
    • Traffic lights
    • Road signs
    • Construction signs, obstructions, and more

  2. Dynamic objects

    • Vehicles (4 wheelers and 2 wheelers)
    • Pedestrians

  3. Ego localization

    • Position
    • Velocity, acceleration
    • Orientation, angular motion

Challenges to perception

  1. Robust detection and segmentation: current machine learning and deep learning technologies can not achieve human level capabilities.
  2. Sensor uncertainty:
    • Visibility can be challenge
    • GPS measurements may get corrupted
    • LIDAR and Radar data are noisy in terms of their positional value
  3. Occlusion, reflection in camera or LIDAR data
  4. Illumination, lens flare on sensor that make sensors unavailable。
  5. Weather, precipitation

Decision making

Planning types (window of time)

  • Long term

    • How to navigate from New York to Los Angeles?

  • Short term:

    • Can I change my lane to the lane right of me?
    • Can I pass this intersection and join the left road?

  • Immediate

    • Can I stay on track on this curved road?
    • Accelerate or brake, by how much?

Type of planning

  1. Reactive planning: rule-based planning. We have rules that take into account the current state of ego and other objects and give decisions.
  2. Predictive planning: Make predictions about other vehicles and how they are moving. Then use these predictions to inform our decisions.
